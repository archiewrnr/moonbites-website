Caffeine and Sleep: Finding Your Real Cutoff Time

Quick takeaways
  • Caffeine's half-life is 5–7 hours on average, but ranges from 2–10 hours between individuals.
  • Caffeine consumed around 6 hours before bedtime has been shown to cut total sleep time by roughly 40 minutes in trials.
  • There's no universal cutoff — genetics affect how fast you clear it, so "no coffee after 2pm" is a reasonable default, not a hard rule for everyone.

Caffeine's half-life — the time it takes your body to clear half of it — averages five to seven hours, but the real range across healthy adults is much wider: some people clear it in two hours, others take up to ten. That variability is largely genetic, tied to how quickly your liver metabolizes it, which is part of why the same afternoon coffee wrecks one person's night and barely registers for another.

Where the research is more consistent is on the downside of cutting it close: caffeine consumed around six hours before bedtime has been shown to reduce sleep efficiency and cut total sleep time by around 40 minutes in controlled trials, even in people who don't subjectively feel "wired." A randomized crossover trial found that smaller doses (around 100mg — roughly one cup of coffee) were tolerated up to about four hours before bed, while larger doses (400mg or more) disrupted sleep even when taken up to twelve hours before bedtime.

The practical takeaway isn't a single magic cutoff time — it's that caffeine lingers far longer than most people assume, and individual sensitivity varies enormously. If you're aiming for a 10pm bedtime, an early-afternoon cutoff is a sensible starting point; if you're still struggling to fall asleep, moving that earlier — or checking for hidden caffeine in tea, chocolate or pre-workout supplements — is worth testing before assuming it's something else.

Further reading: Dose and timing effects of caffeine on subsequent sleep — a randomized clinical crossover trial (SLEEP journal), How long caffeine keeps you awake (Houston Methodist).
