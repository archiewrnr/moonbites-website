The Ideal Bedroom Temperature for Sleep, According to the Science

Quick takeaways
  • Falling asleep depends on your core body temperature dropping — and that requires your hands and feet to warm up first, sending heat out through the skin.
  • Sleep research generally points to 60–67°F (15.5–19.5°C) as the range that best supports this heat-loss process for most adults.
  • A room that's too warm blocks heat dissipation and is linked to less slow-wave sleep, even when you don't consciously notice feeling hot.

Sleep onset isn't just a matter of feeling tired — it's a thermoregulatory event. In the hour or two before you fall asleep, blood vessels in your hands and feet dilate, a process called distal vasodilation, and heat that's been stored in your core gets carried out to your skin and released into the room. Researchers measure this as the distal-proximal skin temperature gradient, or DPG: the difference between how warm your extremities are versus your trunk. Studies tracking this gradient have found it to be one of the strongest predictors of how quickly someone falls asleep — a better predictor, in some analyses, than core temperature itself or even melatonin levels.

This is where room temperature comes in. If the air around you is too warm, your body can't shed heat efficiently through your skin, so the core-temperature drop that sleep depends on gets stalled or blunted. That's the physiological reason a hot bedroom doesn't just feel unpleasant — a higher core body temperature during the night has been associated with less time spent in slow-wave sleep, the deepest and most physically restorative sleep stage, and with more fragmented sleep overall. Based on this mechanism, sleep researchers and organizations like the Sleep Foundation generally recommend keeping bedrooms in the 60–67°F (15.5–19.5°C) range, with around 65°F as a common middle-ground target for most adults.

That range is a starting point, not a rule. Individual variation is real: older adults tend to produce and retain less body heat, so some sleep better modestly warmer, up to the low-to-mid 70s°F. What actually matters is whether your setup lets that hand-and-foot warming happen — breathable bedding, socks if your feet run cold, and a room cool enough that your core can lose heat easily are all pulling toward the same goal, regardless of the exact number on the thermostat.
