Chamomile and Sleep: What the Research Actually Shows

Quick takeaways
  • Chamomile's calming effect is mainly attributed to apigenin, a flavonoid that binds to GABA-A receptors in the brain — the same receptor family targeted by some anti-anxiety medications.
  • A 2024 systematic review of clinical trials found chamomile modestly improved self-reported sleep quality scores, and an earlier meta-analysis of 12 randomized trials reached a similar conclusion.
  • The evidence is thinner for chamomile as a treatment for diagnosed insomnia specifically — NIH's own review notes a lack of strong support there, even though general sleep-quality results look more encouraging.

Chamomile tea before bed is one of the oldest sleep rituals around, and unlike a lot of folk remedies, it has a plausible pharmacological story behind it. Chamomile flowers contain apigenin, a flavonoid believed to bind to GABA-A receptors in the brain — the same inhibitory receptor system that benzodiazepine medications act on, though far more mildly. That binding is thought to produce a gentle calming, anxiety-easing effect, which is the leading explanation for why a cup of chamomile tends to feel relaxing rather than stimulating.

The clinical evidence lines up reasonably well with that story, in a modest way. A 2019 meta-analysis of 12 randomized controlled trials found a significant improvement in sleep quality after chamomile use, and a more recent 2024 systematic review of clinical trials reported a similar reduction in Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores. Several of the underlying trials focused on specific groups — postpartum women, older adults, people with generalized anxiety — rather than the general population, and effect sizes across the literature are consistently small to moderate rather than dramatic.

Where the evidence gets more cautious is insomnia specifically, which is a distinct condition from everyday poor sleep quality. NIH's National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health notes that a review turned up only one trial evaluating chamomile against diagnosed insomnia, and it found no significant benefit. Chamomile is also generally well tolerated — NCCIH considers it likely safe in amounts found in tea — though people with allergies to ragweed, daisies or related plants should be cautious, and it can interact with a small number of medications including warfarin. The honest summary: chamomile looks like a reasonable, low-risk addition to a wind-down routine with some real evidence behind it, not a proven fix for clinical insomnia.

Further reading: Does Chamomile Tea Make You Sleep? (Sleep Foundation), Chamomile: Usefulness and Safety (NIH NCCIH) and Effects of chamomile on sleep: a systematic review and meta-analysis (ScienceDirect).
