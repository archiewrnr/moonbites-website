What Blue Light Actually Does to Your Sleep

Quick takeaways
  • Light in the 450–500nm ("blue") range suppresses melatonin release more than other wavelengths.
  • The effect is real, but often overstated — total darkness before bed isn't required, just moderation.
  • Putting the phone away roughly an hour before bed measurably reduces the impact on sleep onset.

The physiology behind "no screens before bed" advice is well established: light in the blue wavelength range, roughly 450–500 nanometers, suppresses melatonin production more than other colors of light, delays your circadian phase, and prolongs how long it takes to fall asleep. This was first clearly demonstrated in a 2011 study in the Journal of Applied Physiology and has been replicated in various forms since, including reduced deep sleep ratios after pre-bedtime blue light exposure.

More recent research adds useful nuance. A 2023 study in Brain Communications compared young men reading on a phone versus a physical book before bed and found that while blue light did suppress melatonin, the negative effect on sleep was largely avoided if the phone was put away at least an hour before lights-out. There's also a growing, healthy skepticism in the research community about how much of the "phones ruin sleep" effect is really about light wavelength versus the simple fact that scrolling is mentally stimulating — some recent reviews note the two are hard to fully separate in real-world settings.

The practical takeaway sits between the two extremes: blue-light-blocking glasses and night-mode filters aren't magic, but they're not nothing either. The bigger lever is probably the hour-before-bed buffer — putting the phone down, dimming the room, and doing something that isn't actively stimulating, which is also the basis of any good wind-down routine.
