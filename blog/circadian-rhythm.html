Circadian Rhythm 101: How Your Internal Clock Actually Works

Quick takeaways
  • Your circadian rhythm is run by a master clock — the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — in your brain, synced mainly by light hitting your eyes.
  • Body temperature and hormone release follow the same 24-hour pattern; sleep is only the most visible part of it.
  • Irregular light exposure and schedules are what throw it off — not "bad genetics."

Buried in your hypothalamus is a cluster of roughly 20,000 neurons called the suprachiasmatic nucleus, or SCN — your body's master clock. Even in a lab with no external time cues, SCN neurons keep firing in a roughly 24-hour rhythm, peaking around midday and quieting overnight. Light is the strongest signal that keeps this clock synced to the actual day-night cycle outside; a direct neural pathway runs from your eyes to the SCN specifically for this purpose, separate from the visual pathway you see with.

The SCN doesn't just control when you feel sleepy — it choreographs a whole set of daily rhythms. Core body temperature oscillates on the same schedule, dropping to its lowest point around 6am and peaking in the afternoon; that temperature drop is part of what makes falling asleep easier at night than during the day. Melatonin release is timed the same way, ramping up as the SCN registers darkness, which is the entire reason light exposure timing matters so much for sleep.

"Night owl" and "early bird" tendencies are real and largely genetic — but they're variations in the timing of the same underlying clock, not a different system. What reliably disrupts everyone's rhythm, regardless of chronotype, is inconsistent light exposure and irregular schedules: shift work, jet lag, and simply going to bed at wildly different times each night all force the SCN to keep resyncing, which is the actual mechanism behind why consistent sleep and wake times make such a measurable difference.

Further reading: Physiology, Circadian Rhythm (StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf).
